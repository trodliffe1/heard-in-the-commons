Infected Blood Compensation Scheme

The UK Government, led by Paymaster General Nick Thomas-Symonds, has made significant strides in the Infected Blood Compensation Scheme, established in August 2024 following an inquiry that uncovered systemic failures leading to suffering and loss of life. The government has accepted all 12 recommendations from the inquiry and is committed to providing regular updates. Draft regulations for the 2025 scheme have been presented to Parliament, with initial payments expected to reach affected individuals by the end of the year.

To date, over £1 billion in interim payments has been distributed, with 113 individuals invited to claim, resulting in 23 offers totalling over £34 million. The Infected Blood Compensation Authority (IBCA) plans to invite an additional 250 applicants by March, focusing on efficient claims processing and fairness across all groups. An £11.8 billion budget has been allocated to support the initiative.

Despite these efforts, concerns remain about the slow processing of claims, with only 0.2% of potential claimants contacted and two victims reportedly dying each week without compensation. Bipartisan support is emerging for enhancing processing capacity, as the government anticipates a surge in claims. The Minister has been urged to clarify whether current funding will cover the expected 140,000 claims. Additionally, legislation on a duty of candour for public officials is expected by April 15 to prevent future scandals. The IBCA is also working on a compensation calculator to assist claimants and streamline the process.
